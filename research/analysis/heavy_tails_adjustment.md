## 2. The "Heavy Tails" Mechanism Needs Refinement
**Status: ADJUSTMENT NEEDED**

The paper claims (Section 4.3 & 6.3) that complementary production ($\rho < 0$) inherently generates heavy-tailed (Pareto) distributions via the q-exponential equilibrium.

**The Discrepancy:**
*   **Simulation (Test 2):** Deterministic optimization of a complementary economy ($\rho < 0$) leads to **equality** (Thin Tails, Gini ~0.09). Resources are diverted to "bottleneck" firms to maximize aggregate output.
*   **Paper's Logic:** Relies on *MaxEnt* (Statistical Equilibrium), effectively assuming a "high temperature" economy where entropy dominates.

**The Adjustment:**
The theory must explicitly distinguish between **Ground State (Optimization)** and **Thermodynamic State (Equilibrium)**.

*   **Zero Temperature ($T \to 0$):** In a perfectly efficient, noise-free economy, complementarity leads to equality (Leontief-like uniform distribution).
*   **High Temperature ($T > 0$):** In a noisy, entropic economy, complementarity *plus* entropy leads to heavy tails. The "bottleneck" firms become critical nodes that cannot fail, while the entropy function spreads the probability mass.

**Correction to Prediction 5:**
*   *Old:* "Complementary sectors have heavier tails."
*   *New:* "Complementary sectors have heavier tails **only in the presence of significant idiosyncratic shocks (Temperature)**. In highly optimized/planned sectors, complementarity leads to compressed distributions."

## 3. Integration with Gibrat's Law
The current theory attempts to derive tails solely from static production technology. It ignores **Gibrat's Law** (stochastic proportional growth), which is the standard explanation for Pareto tails.

**Recommendation:**
Frame the CES $\rho$ not as the *generator* of the tail, but as the **selection pressure** acting on the tail generated by Gibrat's Law.
*   **Gibrat's Law** generates the candidate distribution (log-normal/Pareto).
*   **CES Technology** filters this distribution.
    *   If $\rho > 0$ (Substitutes): The market amplifies the tail (Winner-Take-All).
    *   If $\rho < 0$ (Complements): The market dampens the tail (props up losers).

**Synthesis:**
The observed heavy tails in reality likely come from Gibrat's Law ($T$) overpowering the equality-forcing nature of complementarity ($\rho < 0$). The theory should express the tail exponent $\zeta$ as a function of *both* the production elasticity $\sigma$ and the "economic temperature" (growth volatility).
