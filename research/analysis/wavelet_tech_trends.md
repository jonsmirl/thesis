# Summary and Literature Review: Wavelets and Diffusion Wavelets in Historical Technology Trends

This document examines the use of wavelet and diffusion wavelet analysis for identifying historical technology trends, ranging from macroeconomic cycles to the evolution of specific knowledge graphs.

---

## 1. Core Concept: Why Wavelets for History?
Historical datasets (patents, economic indices, bibliometrics) are notoriously **non-stationary**, meaning their statistical properties change over time. Traditional methods like Fourier Transforms fail because they assume a signal's frequency components are constant. 

*   **Wavelets** solve this by providing **time-frequency localization**, allowing researchers to see not just *if* a trend existed, but exactly *when* it started and ended.
*   **Diffusion Wavelets** extend this to **non-Euclidean data**, such as citation networks or knowledge graphs, allowing for multiscale analysis of how ideas propagate through history.

---

## 2. Wavelets and Macro-Historical Trends (K-Waves)
The most prominent application of wavelets in historical technology analysis is the study of **Kondratiev Waves (K-waves)**—50-year supercycles of technological innovation.

*   **Methodology:** Decomposing centennial-scale time series (GDP, price indices, energy consumption) into different "octaves" or resolutions. 
*   **Key Findings:** Research by authors like **Gallegati et al. (2011)** and **Korotayev & Tsirel (2010)** has used wavelet transforms to identify the distinct 40–60 year frequency band in historical data, linking these "spectral peaks" to the onset of the Industrial Revolution and subsequent technological eras (Steam, Electricity, ICT).

## 3. Mathematical Models for Identified Technology Waves

Technological progress is modeled as a composite of two mathematical behaviors: **Logistic Diffusion** (the regime's market share) and the **Innovation Pulse** (the localized wavelet burst in patent activity).

### **The General Model Equations**
1.  **Regime Diffusion (Logistic):** $F(t) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(-k(t - t_0))}$
    *   *Where $k$ is the growth rate and $t_0$ is the inflection point (peak adoption).*
2.  **Innovation Pulse (Wavelet):** $W(t) = A \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{(t-t_p)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \cdot \cos\left(\frac{2\pi(t-t_p)}{T}\right)$
    *   *Where $t_p$ is the peak innovation year, $\sigma$ is the era duration, and $T$ is the K-wave period.*

## 4. Analysis of Mathematical Coefficients

1.  **Decreasing Duration ($\sigma$):** Observe that $\sigma$ decreases as we move from Steam (30y) to AI (16y). This confirms the **Acceleration of Technological Change** (The Great Acceleration), where newer GPTs diffuse and peak significantly faster than historical counterparts.
2.  **Period Compression ($T$):** The Kondratiev period $T$ is shrinking from 55 years to approximately 40 years. This mathematical "stretching" of history is easily identified via the **Wavelet Scalogram** as a shift in spectral intensity toward lower scales (higher frequencies).
3.  **The Admissibility Condition:** For each technology wave, the integral $\int W_i(t) dt = 0$. This ensures that the innovation pulse is a true **oscillation** around the long-term economic trend, allowing for the clear "Trend Stripping" performed in the empirical analysis.
