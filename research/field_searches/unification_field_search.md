## Framework 1: Multi-Type Next-Generation Matrix (Master R₀)

**Source:** Diekmann, Heesterbeek & Metz (1990); Van den Driessche & Watmough (2002); Diekmann, Heesterbeek & Roberts (2010), *J. R. Soc. Interface* 7:873–885.

**The Math:**

Consider a system with *n* "infectious" types (here: layers of the mesh economy). Decompose the Jacobian at the trivial steady state as:

$$J = T + \Sigma$$

where $T$ is the *transmission matrix* (amplification: investment flows, network recruitment, autocatalytic training, settlement feedback) and $\Sigma$ is the *transition matrix* (damping: depreciation, agent exit, learning curve saturation, Triffin squeeze). The **next-generation matrix** is:

$$K = -T\Sigma^{-1}$$

The entry $K_{ij}$ gives the expected number of "type-j infections" generated by a single "type-i infected" over its lifetime. The **master reproduction number** is:

$$\mathcal{R}_0 = \rho(K) = \text{spectral radius of } K$$

The system undergoes a transcritical bifurcation at $\mathcal{R}_0 = 1$. For $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$, the trivial equilibrium (no mesh) is stable. For $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$, a non-trivial equilibrium (active mesh economy) emerges.

**Variable Mapping:**

Define four "types" corresponding to the four feedback loops:

| Type | State Variable | Amplification (T) | Damping (Σ) |
|------|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1: Silicon learning | Cost reduction rate | Investment → learning curve | Learning curve saturation |
| 2: Mesh formation | Network density N | Recruitment via diversity premium | Agent exit, coordination costs |
| 3: Autocatalytic growth | Capability φ | Self-referential training | Baumol bottleneck |
| 4: Settlement feedback | Stablecoin share S | Treasury demand → settlement quality | Triffin squeeze on safe assets |

The 4×4 next-generation matrix $K$ has off-diagonal entries that capture **cross-layer amplification**:

- $K_{21}$: cheaper silicon enables more mesh nodes (Paper 1 → Paper 2)
- $K_{32}$: denser mesh enables autocatalytic training (Paper 2 → Paper 4)
- $K_{43}$: higher mesh capability drives stablecoin demand (Paper 4 → Paper 5)
- $K_{14}$: better settlement infrastructure attracts investment (Paper 5 → Paper 1)

The individual paper R₀'s are **type-reproduction numbers**—the spectral radius restricted to a single type with the other types held fixed. The master $\mathcal{R}_0 = \rho(K)$ is strictly larger than any individual type-reproduction number when cross-layer amplification is positive (which it is by construction). This means the system can be sub-threshold in every individual layer yet super-threshold in aggregate—a genuine multi-layer emergent phenomenon.

**Unifies:** Pattern 1 (R₀ thresholds), Pattern 4 (self-reinforcing loops), Pattern 5 (scale progression—the matrix structure encodes the cascade)

**Coverage:** 4/5 papers (Papers 1, 2, 4, 5 directly; Paper 3 enters through the settlement layer)

**Cannot Do:** Does not explain the phase transition character (first-order vs. continuous), the CES triple role, or the universality question. It is a linear stability tool; it identifies *when* transitions happen but not *how* (the nonlinear structure of the transition itself).

**Assessment:** 8/10 for unification potential of the R₀ pattern specifically. This is the cleanest and most rigorous answer to Q1.

## Framework 2: Haken's Synergetics (Order Parameters + Slaving Principle)

**Source:** Haken (1977, 1983), *Synergetics: An Introduction* and *Advanced Synergetics*, Springer; Haken & Wunderlin (1982), Z. Phys. B 47:179–187.

**The Math:**

Consider a high-dimensional dynamical system $\dot{q} = N(q, \alpha)$ where $q$ is a state vector and $\alpha$ is a control parameter. Near an instability point $\alpha = \alpha_c$:

1. **Linear stability analysis** decomposes modes into *unstable* (slow, growing) and *stable* (fast, damped):
$$\dot{u} = \lambda_u u + g_u(u, s)$$
$$\dot{s} = \lambda_s s + g_s(u, s)$$
where $\lambda_u \approx 0$ (marginal/unstable) and $\lambda_s \ll 0$ (strongly damped).

2. **Slaving principle**: For $|\lambda_s| \gg |\lambda_u|$, the stable modes adiabatically follow the unstable modes: $s = h(u)$, reducing the system to the **order parameter equation**:
$$\dot{u} = \lambda_u u + \text{nonlinear terms in } u$$

3. Near the instability, the order parameter equation takes **normal form** depending on the bifurcation type. For transcritical bifurcation (relevant to all five papers):
$$\dot{u} = (\alpha - \alpha_c)u - u^2$$

**Variable Mapping:**

| Paper | Control Parameter α | Order Parameter u | Slaved Modes s |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1 | Cumulative hyperscaler capex | Cost advantage of distributed | Individual fab learning rates |
| 2 | Edge device density | Giant component fraction | Individual agent specializations |
| 3 | Fiat quality gap | Stablecoin adoption fraction | Individual corridor costs |
| 4 | Mesh diversity (N) | φ_eff (effective automation) | Individual agent training states |
| 5 | Mesh-to-market coupling η | Settlement quality S | Individual market participant states |

**The ceiling-from-above structure (Pattern 4) is the slaving principle applied hierarchically:**

At each level, the order parameter of level *n* becomes a *control parameter* for level *n+1*. But the order parameter cannot grow without bound because it is itself slaved to a *constraint from level n+1* that acts as a slow ceiling. Formally:

- Level 1 order parameter $u_1$ grows until it saturates at a value determined by a constraint that is the control parameter for level 2.
- Level 2's order parameter $u_2$ then becomes active, grows until constrained, activating level 3.
- And so on.

This is **exactly** the structure described in Pattern 4, and it explains why each paper's amplifying loop hits a ceiling from the next layer.

**Unifies:** Pattern 4 (self-limiting loops—this is the slaving principle across hierarchical levels), Pattern 1 (R₀ crossings are instability points where new order parameters emerge), Pattern 5 (scale progression is the hierarchical order parameter cascade)

**Coverage:** 5/5 papers (all are described as instabilities with order parameters)

**Cannot Do:** Synergetics is qualitative about the *specific* functional forms—it tells you that near instability the dynamics reduce to order parameter equations, but doesn't tell you the specific form of those equations without detailed microscopic modeling. It doesn't explain the CES triple role. It doesn't explain universality (though Haken himself connected synergetics to RG theory).

**Assessment:** 7/10. Provides the conceptual skeleton for the hierarchical structure but needs the mathematical flesh from other frameworks.

## Framework 3: Geometric Singular Perturbation Theory (Multi-Timescale Dynamics)

**Source:** Fenichel (1979), *J. Differential Equations* 31:53–98; Cardin & Teixeira (2017), *SIAM J. Appl. Dyn. Syst.* 16:1425–1452 (extension to *n* timescales); Jones (1995), *Lecture Notes in Mathematics* 1609.

**The Math:**

A system with *n* timescales is written:

$$\epsilon_1 \dot{x}_1 = f_1(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$$
$$\epsilon_2 \dot{x}_2 = f_2(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$$
$$\vdots$$
$$\dot{x}_n = f_n(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$$

where $0 < \epsilon_1 \ll \epsilon_2 \ll \cdots \ll 1$. Fenichel's theorem guarantees that for $\epsilon_i$ sufficiently small, there exists a **slow manifold** $\mathcal{M}_\epsilon$ close to the **critical manifold** $\mathcal{M}_0 = \{x : f_i(x) = 0 \text{ for all fast } i\}$, and the dynamics on $\mathcal{M}_\epsilon$ are a smooth perturbation of the reduced (slow) dynamics.

**Variable Mapping:**

The five papers have a natural timescale separation:

| Variable | Timescale | ε parameter |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Market microstructure (η, λ) | Milliseconds–hours | ε₁ ~ 10⁻⁶ |
| Stablecoin/settlement (S, b) | Days–weeks | ε₂ ~ 10⁻³ |
| Mesh formation (N, specialization) | Weeks–months | ε₃ ~ 10⁻² |
| Autocatalytic capability (φ) | Months–years | ε₄ ~ 10⁻¹ |
| Learning curves, institutional change | Years–decades | ε₅ ~ 1 (slow) |

The five-paper system is a **five-timescale singular perturbation problem**. On the fastest timescale (market microstructure), all other variables are approximately frozen. On the slowest timescale (institutional adaptation), all faster variables have equilibrated on their respective slow manifolds.

This formalizes the **ceiling-from-above structure**: the fast variables (amplification loops) equilibrate on the slow manifold defined by the slow variables (constraints). The "ceiling" is literally the slow manifold surface $f_{\text{fast}}(x_{\text{fast}}, x_{\text{slow}}) = 0$. When the slow variable drifts far enough that the slow manifold loses normal hyperbolicity (a fold or transcritical bifurcation of the fast system), the system undergoes a **canard explosion** or **relaxation oscillation**—a rapid transition to a new branch. This is the R₀ = 1 crossing.

**Unifies:** Pattern 4 (ceiling structure = slow manifold), Pattern 1 (R₀ crossings = loss of normal hyperbolicity), Pattern 5 (scale progression = timescale hierarchy)

**Coverage:** 4/5 papers (does not directly address Paper 3's empirical structure, which is cross-sectional rather than dynamic)

**Cannot Do:** Like synergetics, this is a framework for *analyzing* dynamical systems, not a specific model. It requires the explicit ODEs from each paper as input. It does not explain the CES triple role or universality.

**Assessment:** 7/10. The right mathematical language for the multi-scale dynamics, and combined with the NGM (Framework 1) it provides a rigorous treatment.

## Framework 4: Interdependent Networks / Network of Networks

**Source:** Buldyrev, Parshani, Paul, Stanley & Havlin (2010), *Nature* 464:1025–1028; Gao, Buldyrev, Stanley & Havlin (2012), *Nature Physics* 8:40–48; Kivelä et al. (2014), *J. Complex Networks* 2:203–271.

**The Math:**

For two interdependent networks A and B with degree distributions $P_A(k)$ and $P_B(k)$, coupled by dependency links, the mutual giant component satisfies the self-consistency equations:

$$x = p \cdot g_A(g_B(x) \cdot p) \cdot p$$

where $g_A, g_B$ are the generating functions of the degree distributions. The critical percolation threshold $p_c$ is found where the non-trivial solution first appears.

Key result: In fully interdependent networks, the percolation transition changes from **second-order** (continuous, as in isolated networks) to **first-order** (discontinuous, catastrophic). Reducing coupling strength gradually changes the transition back to second-order at a tricritical point.

**Variable Mapping:**

| Network Layer | Nodes | Links | Dependency Links |
|--------------|-------|-------|-----------------|
| Device layer (Paper 2) | AI agents | Task collaboration | Hardware dependency on silicon (Paper 1) |
| Knowledge layer (Paper 4) | Training datasets | Autocatalytic training | Capability dependency on mesh (Paper 2) |
| Financial layer (Paper 5) | Market participants | Transaction links | Settlement dependency on stablecoins |
| Monetary layer (Paper 3) | Countries/corridors | Trade links | Currency dependency on fiat quality |

The transition from second-order to first-order percolation as coupling increases maps directly onto the transition character differences across the papers: weakly coupled layers show continuous transitions (Paper 1's gradual learning curve), while strongly coupled layers show catastrophic transitions (Paper 5's abrupt settlement feedback, the Triffin squeeze).

**Unifies:** Pattern 3 (phase transitions—explains *why* some are first-order and others continuous), Pattern 1 (percolation thresholds across layers), Pattern 4 (cascade failures propagate across layers)

**Coverage:** 4/5 papers (Paper 3 fits less naturally as it's empirical/cross-sectional)

**Cannot Do:** Does not explain the CES triple role. The specific models are for random network failures, not the endogenous growth dynamics that drive the papers. The mapping requires significant adaptation.

**Assessment:** 6/10. Valuable for understanding the transition character and cascading dynamics, but not the primary unifying framework.

## Framework 6: The CES Triple Role Theorem (Novel Construction)

**Source:** No single reference. This is constructed from properties of concave aggregators established in: Arrow, Chenery, Minhas & Solow (1961); Dixit & Stiglitz (1977); Jones (2005), *The Shape of Production Functions and the Direction of Technical Change*, NBER WP 11606; results from portfolio theory (Markowitz 1952) and information theory (Cover & Thomas 2006).

**The Math:**

**Theorem (CES Triple Role).** Let $Y = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} a_i x_i^\rho\right)^{1/\rho}$ with $\rho < 1$ (elasticity of substitution $\sigma = 1/(1-\rho) > 1$) and $a_i > 0$, $\sum a_i = 1$. Then $\rho < 1$ simultaneously implies:

**(a) Superadditivity (Diversity Premium):** For any partition of inputs into groups, the aggregate output exceeds the sum of group outputs. Specifically, $Y$ is strictly concave in the vector $(x_1, \ldots, x_N)$, so by Jensen's inequality, a mean-preserving spread in the $x_i$ (greater heterogeneity) *increases* $Y$ when $\rho < 1$. This is the diversity premium: heterogeneous specialists produce more in aggregate than homogeneous generalists.

Formally: for $\rho < 1$, $f(x) = x^\rho$ is concave, so:
$$\left(\sum a_i x_i^\rho\right)^{1/\rho} \geq \left(\sum a_i\right)^{1/\rho} \cdot \bar{x}$$
with equality only when all $x_i$ are equal. Greater variance in $x_i$ *increases* $Y$.

**(b) Collapse Resistance (Informational Robustness):** If inputs are correlated (model collapse scenario where $x_i \to x_j$ for many pairs), then effective $N$ falls. When $\rho < 1$, the output is *more sensitive* to reductions in effective variety than when $\rho > 1$. But the converse holds: a system that *maintains* diversity under $\rho < 1$ is *more robust* to any single-input failure than one with $\rho > 1$, because no single input dominates the aggregate. The effective number of independent inputs is:

$$N_{\text{eff}} = \left(\sum a_i^{1/(1-\rho)}\right)^{1-\rho}$$

which is maximized when the $a_i$ are equal and decreases with correlation. When $\rho < 1$, the marginal product of the $N_{\text{eff}}$-th variety is *always positive*, creating an endogenous incentive to maintain diversity.

**(c) Collusion Resistance (Competitive Independence):** Under CES demand with $\rho < 1$ (goods are complements), a coalition of $k$ producers attempting to restrict output faces a decreasing residual demand elasticity. The complementarity means that restricting one input *reduces the value of all other inputs*, making collusion less attractive than under substitutability ($\rho > 1$). Formally, in a Cournot oligopoly with CES demand, the incentive compatibility constraint for a cartel is:

$$\pi_{\text{deviate}} - \pi_{\text{collude}} \propto (\sigma - 1) = \rho/(1-\rho)$$

For $\rho < 1$, $\sigma > 1$, and the deviation incentive is positive, meaning collusion is *harder to sustain* when inputs are complementary. (This connects to Bernheim & Whinston (1990) on multimarket contact—complementarity across markets destabilizes collusion.)

**The unifying principle:** All three properties follow from a single mathematical fact: **the CES aggregator with $\rho < 1$ is a strictly concave function on the positive orthant**. Concavity implies (a) Jensen's inequality → diversity premium, (b) positive marginal products for all inputs → robustness to loss of any input, and (c) cross-input complementarity → deviation incentive against collusion.

**Unifies:** Pattern 2 (CES triple role) across Papers 2, 4, and 5.

**Coverage:** 3/5 papers (the three that use CES aggregation)

**Cannot Do:** This is a *theorem about a functional form*, not a dynamical model. It does not explain R₀ thresholds, phase transitions, or scale progression.

**Assessment:** 9/10 for the specific question of the CES triple role. This IS the answer to Q3. The triple role is a single theorem, not three separate results. **This is publishable as a standalone result.**

## Framework 7: Fortuin-Kasteleyn Partition Function (Internal to Paper 2, Extensible)

**Source:** Fortuin & Kasteleyn (1972), *Physica* 57:536; Grimmett (2006), *The Random-Cluster Model*, Springer.

**The Math:**

The FK random cluster model unifies percolation ($q = 1$) and the Potts model ($q = 2, 3, \ldots$) through a single partition function:

$$Z_{RC} = \sum_{\omega \in \{0,1\}^E} q^{C(\omega)} \prod_{e \in E} p^{\omega(e)} (1-p)^{1-\omega(e)}$$

where $C(\omega)$ is the number of connected components, $p$ is the bond probability, and $q$ interpolates between percolation and Potts models. For $q \leq 4$ in 2D, the transition is continuous; for $q > 4$, it is first-order.

**Mapping to Papers 1–2:** Paper 2 already uses this framework explicitly. The mesh formation is percolation ($q = 1$), and specialization is Potts crystallization ($q > 2$, first-order). The FK partition function is the *internal* unification of Paper 2.

**Extensibility to other papers:** Limited. The FK framework describes *equilibrium* statistical mechanics on a fixed network. Papers 1, 4, and 5 describe *driven* dynamical systems far from equilibrium. The FK partition function cannot accommodate endogenous growth (Paper 4) or coupled ODE dynamics (Paper 5).

**Unifies:** Pattern 3 (phase transitions) *within* Paper 2

**Coverage:** 1.5/5 papers (Paper 2 fully, Paper 1's BEC dissolution can be reframed as an FK model at $q \to 0$)

**Cannot Do:** Dynamical phenomena, endogenous growth, settlement feedback, CES aggregation

**Assessment:** 4/10 for cross-paper unification. Excellent *within* Paper 2 but not extensible.

## Framework 8: Transcritical Bifurcation Normal Form (Generic Result)

**Source:** Strogatz (1994), *Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos*; Kuznetsov (2004), *Elements of Applied Bifurcation Theory*; Guckenheimer & Holmes (1983).

**The Math:**

The transcritical bifurcation has the normal form:
$$\dot{x} = \mu x - x^2$$

where $\mu$ is the bifurcation parameter. For $\mu < 0$, $x = 0$ is stable and $x = \mu$ is unstable. For $\mu > 0$, the stability switches: $x = 0$ is unstable and $x = \mu$ is stable. The transition at $\mu = 0$ involves an *exchange of stability* between two equilibria.

**This is the generic bifurcation for any system where:**
1. A trivial equilibrium $x = 0$ exists for all parameter values
2. A non-trivial equilibrium exists and exchanges stability with the trivial one
3. The system has a basic symmetry: $x = 0$ is always a fixed point

All five papers satisfy these conditions. The R₀ = 1 crossing IS the transcritical bifurcation in every case.

**Assessment of depth:** This is a **shallow** unification. The transcritical bifurcation is the generic codimension-one bifurcation for systems with a trivially invariant subspace. Saying that all five papers exhibit transcritical bifurcations is like saying that all five papers involve differential equations—true but not informative. The *interesting* question is whether the specific structure of each transcritical bifurcation (the nonlinear terms, the interaction with other variables, the subcritical vs. supercritical character) shares common properties. That requires the specific frameworks above.

**Assessment:** 3/10. True but trivial. This is the "honest negative" component: the R₀ pattern, taken alone, is generic and does not indicate deep unification.

## Framework 9: Hierarchical Semi-Endogenous Growth (Economics-Internal)

**Source:** Jones (1995), *J. Political Economy* 103:759–784; Aghion & Howitt (1992), *Econometrica* 60:323–351; Aghion, Jones & Jones (2024), *NBER WP*.

**The Math:**

Model the five papers as a hierarchical production function:

$$Y = F(A_1, A_2, A_3, A_4, A_5)$$

where $A_i$ is the "technology level" at layer $i$, with dynamics:

$$\dot{A}_i = \delta_i A_i^{\phi_i} H_i^{\lambda_i} \cdot g_i(A_{i-1}, A_{i+1})$$

The function $g_i(A_{i-1}, A_{i+1})$ captures the cross-layer coupling: $A_{i-1}$ enables growth at layer $i$ (amplification), while $A_{i+1}$ constrains it (ceiling). The key parameter is $\phi_i$—the knowledge spillover elasticity at each layer:

- $\phi_i < 1$: Semi-endogenous growth (Paper 4's convergence regime)
- $\phi_i = 1$: Endogenous growth (Paper 4's exponential regime)
- $\phi_i > 1$: Accelerating growth toward singularity (Paper 4's singularity regime)

**This reduces the five papers to a five-equation growth system** in which the interaction structure $g_i$ determines the dynamics.

**Unifies:** Pattern 4 (ceilings = $g_i$ constraints), Pattern 5 (scale hierarchy = production chain)

**Coverage:** 4/5 papers (Paper 3 requires separate treatment as a development economics model)

**Cannot Do:** Does not explain phase transitions (growth models are typically smooth), the CES triple role (unless CES is explicitly used for the aggregation $F$), or universality.

**Assessment:** 6/10. Stays within economics but loses the statistical mechanics insights that make the papers distinctive.

## SYNTHESIS

### What is the strongest candidate for unification?

No single framework achieves full 5/5 unification. The strongest composite candidate is:

**The Synergetic Next-Generation Matrix (SNGM)**: Combine Framework 1 (multi-type NGM for the master R₀), Framework 2 (synergetics for the hierarchical order parameter cascade), and Framework 3 (singular perturbation theory for the timescale separation). This triple framework:

- Derives the master R₀ as $\rho(K)$ where $K$ is the 4×4 next-generation matrix across layers (Q1 answered)
- Explains the hierarchical ceiling structure via the slaving principle: each layer's order parameter becomes the next layer's control parameter (Q4 answered)
- Formalizes the timescale separation via Fenichel's invariant manifold theory, with the slow manifold constraints providing the "ceilings" (Q4 answered rigorously)
- Admits the CES Triple Role Theorem (Framework 6) as an independent result that explains why diversity is simultaneously productive, robust, and competitive (Q3 answered)

### Is full unification (5/5 papers) achievable?

**No**, in the sense of a single partition function or a single set of equations from which all five papers are derivable. The papers span genuinely different mathematical structures:

- Papers 1–2 involve equilibrium/near-equilibrium statistical mechanics (BEC, percolation, Potts)
- Paper 3 is primarily empirical with a development economics structure
- Paper 4 involves nonequilibrium growth dynamics (ODE system with regime transitions)
- Paper 5 involves coupled nonlinear dynamics with financial market microstructure

These are connected by the *same patterns* but not by the *same model*. The connection is tighter than analogy—the NGM genuinely links their threshold structures, and the slaving principle genuinely explains the hierarchical coupling—but it is looser than derivation from a single Lagrangian.

**Partial unification at 4/5 (excluding Paper 3) is the realistic ceiling for a single mathematical paper.** Paper 3's contribution is primarily empirical and enters the unified framework through the settlement layer of the NGM rather than through its own internal dynamics.

### What is the master R₀?

$$\mathcal{R}_0 = \rho(K) \quad \text{where} \quad K = -T\Sigma^{-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 4}$$

with rows/columns indexed by {silicon learning, mesh formation, autocatalytic growth, settlement feedback}. The individual papers' R₀'s are the type-reproduction numbers $T_i = K_{ii} + \sum_{j \neq i} K_{ij} K_{ji} / (1 - K_{jj})$ (approximate, for weakly coupled layers). The master R₀ exceeds all individual R₀'s when cross-layer amplification is positive.

**Eigenvalue interpretation:** The dominant eigenvector of $K$ gives the *composition* of the self-sustaining mode—the relative contribution of each layer to the self-reinforcing cycle. If silicon learning dominates, the eigenvector loads on type 1; if settlement feedback dominates, it loads on type 4.

### Is the CES triple role a single theorem?

**Yes.** The triple role follows from the strict concavity of $f(x) = x^\rho$ for $\rho < 1$:

1. Jensen's inequality → diversity premium (superadditivity)
2. Positive marginal products → collapse resistance (every input matters)
3. Cross-complementarity → collusion resistance (restricting one input hurts all others)

This can be stated and proved as a single proposition. **It is publishable independent of the unified theory**, potentially as a short note in *AER: Insights* or *Journal of Economic Theory*.

### Is the honest negative the right answer?

**Partially.** The R₀ pattern, taken alone, IS generic (Framework 8, score 3/10). Any system with a trivially invariant subspace and a non-trivial equilibrium will exhibit a transcritical bifurcation. Pointing out that five systems all have transcritical bifurcations is not deep.

BUT the *specific structure* of the bifurcations is NOT generic:
- The cross-layer coupling (NGM) is a specific, quantifiable relationship
- The hierarchical ceiling structure (synergetics) is not generic—it requires specific timescale separation and order parameter coupling
- The CES triple role is a specific mathematical result, not a generic feature of threshold systems
- The FK unification within Paper 2 (percolation + Potts as one partition function) is a deep mathematical result from statistical physics

**The honest answer is: the unity is neither deep (single model) nor shallow (mere analogy). It is at an intermediate level—structural.** The five papers share structural properties (NGM threshold structure, hierarchical slaving, CES complementarity) that are specific enough to formalize as theorems but not specific enough to derive from a single model.

### What is the minimal unified model?

A **four-ODE system with CES coupling** that nests the five papers as limiting cases:

$$\dot{c} = \delta_c I^{\alpha} c^{\phi_c} - \gamma_c c \qquad \text{(silicon learning, Paper 1)}$$

$$\dot{N} = \beta(c) N \left(1 - N/N^*(c)\right) - \mu N \qquad \text{(mesh formation, Paper 2)}$$

$$\dot{\phi} = \frac{\phi_0}{1 - \beta_{\text{auto}} \phi_0} \left(\sum a_i x_i^\rho\right)^{1/\rho} - \delta_\phi \phi \qquad \text{(autocatalysis, Paper 4)}$$

$$\dot{S} = \eta(\phi, N) S (1 - S/\bar{S}(\phi)) - \nu S \qquad \text{(settlement, Paper 5)}$$

where $c$ = distributed cost advantage, $N$ = mesh density, $\phi$ = capability, $S$ = stablecoin share. The CES term in the $\dot{\phi}$ equation is the diversity-weighted aggregate of mesh outputs. The carrying capacities $N^*(c)$ and $\bar{S}(\phi)$ are the ceilings imposed by adjacent layers.

The master R₀ is the spectral radius of the Jacobian $T\Sigma^{-1}$ evaluated at the trivial steady state $(c, N, \phi, S) = (0, 0, 0, 0)$.

Paper 3 enters through the parameter $\eta(\phi, N)$—the rate at which mesh capability translates into stablecoin demand, mediated by the fiat quality gap across countries.

## ANSWER TO Q2: IS THERE A UNIVERSALITY CLASS?

The transitions in the five papers do **not** belong to a single universality class. They include:

- **Mean-field percolation** (Paper 2, continuous, β = 1, γ = 1, ν = 1/2)
- **Mean-field Potts, q > 2** (Paper 2 specialization, first-order—no universality class in the traditional sense)
- **Transcritical bifurcation** (Papers 1, 4, 5—this is a codimension-1 bifurcation, not a phase transition with critical exponents)
- **BEC dissolution** (Paper 1—this is a condensation transition, mean-field with different exponents from percolation)

The mesh paper's argument that mean-field exponents are exact on small-world networks (spectral dimension > 4) is correct and applies to Paper 2's percolation transition. But the other papers don't have phase transitions in the strict statistical mechanics sense—they have bifurcations of ODE systems, which have no critical exponents.

**The universality question dissolves**: the recurrence of threshold crossings is explained by the generic transcritical bifurcation (shallow) and the specific NGM structure (deep), not by shared universality class.
